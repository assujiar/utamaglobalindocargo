# Content Voice & Copy System

The following guidelines define the tone, style and vocabulary for all
copywriting in the rebuilt UGC website.  They ensure that content feels
human, sharp, credible and consistent across pages, languages and formats.

## Brand Voice Principles

1. **Operator‑grade clarity** – Write like a seasoned logistics professional
   speaking to business executives.  Use concrete nouns and verbs.  Explain
   processes plainly.  Avoid jargon unless it is standard in shipping and
   supply chain contexts (e.g. FCL, LCL, HS Code).
2. **Human and honest** – Acknowledge uncertainties and constraints.  If a
   service depends on external factors (e.g. customs clearance times), say
   so.  Show empathy for client challenges.  Use second person (“Anda”) in
   Indonesian to address the reader directly; in English use “you”.  When
   appropriate, use natural particles like “kok”, “sih”, “dong” to soften
   statements but do so sparingly to maintain professionalism.
3. **Confident but not boastful** – Convey competence through details and
   examples rather than big claims.  Use first person plural (“kami” / “we”)
   to indicate teamwork and responsibility.  Avoid superlatives (“terbaik”,
   “#1”, “paling inovatif”).
4. **Crisp and varied rhythm** – Mix short, punchy sentences with longer,
   descriptive ones.  Avoid monotonous patterns.  Use rhetorical questions
   occasionally to engage readers, but answer them quickly.  Read aloud
   copy to check for natural flow.
5. **Localized professionalism** – Keep Indonesian copy smart‑casual with
   localised expressions.  Translate idioms sensibly.  Do not write in a
   formal legal tone unless required (e.g. privacy policy).  English copy
   should be clear and straightforward.

## Homepage Voice Rules

* **Headline** – The hero headline should be short, bold and benefit‑oriented.
  Avoid fluff like “Welcome to our website”.
* **Sub‑headline** – Expand on the value proposition in one or two sentences.
  Mention key services (domestic distribution, international freight, customs
  brokerage, warehousing, project cargo).  Use everyday language.
* **Trust strip** – Write a one‑sentence statement that names industries served
  and underscores reliability (“Dipercaya oleh perusahaan manufaktur, FMCG,
  e‑commerce hingga farmasi”).  Do not exaggerate.
* **Section headers** – Use action‑oriented verbs (“Layanan kami”, “Bagaimana
  kami bekerja”) instead of generic labels (“Services”, “Process”).
* **CTA buttons** – Use imperative verbs (“Diskusikan kebutuhan Anda”,
  “Explore services”, “Pelajari lebih jauh”).  Keep to 2–4 words.

## Service Page Voice Rules

* **Category intro** – Begin with a concise description of the category’s
  purpose and when it is relevant.  Make the reader feel understood (“Jika
  pengiriman Anda belum cukup untuk satu truk penuh, LTL adalah pilihan
  efisien”).
* **Sub‑service descriptions** – Use the pattern: what it is, how it works,
  why it matters.  Break content into paragraphs or bullet lists.  Include
  examples (e.g. types of cargo, routes).
* **Key points & best for lists** – Start each bullet with a verb (“Koordinasi
  stuffing di lokasi Anda”, “Bayar hanya ruang yang dipakai”).  Keep bullets
  parallel in structure.  Limit lists to 4–5 items.
* **Process & SLA** – Explain the sequence of events and typical timelines.
  Use numbers only when approximate or verified (e.g. “estimasi 3–5 hari
  kerja”).
* **CTA** – Conclude each sub‑service or page with a clear call to discuss
  needs or get a quote.  Do not promise instant quotes unless that is a
  business capability.

## FAQ Voice Rules

* **Question phrasing** – Use real questions from customers.  Begin with “Apa”
  (“What”), “Bagaimana” (“How”), “Berapa lama” (“How long”), etc.  Avoid
  rhetorical questions that lead to sales pitches.
* **Answer style** – Answer directly in the first sentence.  Provide
  elaboration if necessary.  Use polite, conversational tone.  If the answer
  depends on variables, state what factors influence the outcome.
* **Length** – Keep answers under 150 words where possible.  Break long
  answers into paragraphs or lists.

## CTA Writing Rules

* **Clarity** – Say exactly what will happen when the user clicks (“Kirim
  permintaan konsultasi”, “Hubungi via WhatsApp”).  Avoid generic “Submit”.
* **Urgency without pressure** – Convey that action is worthwhile but not
  forced.  Use phrases like “Diskusikan sekarang”, not “Order now” or “Act
  fast”.
* **Consistency** – Use the same CTA wording for similar actions across the
  site.  For example, all contact CTAs should contain “Diskusikan”/“Discuss”.

## Trust & Proof Writing Rules

* **Facts first** – Lead with the challenge, then explain the solution and
  results.  Use approximate metrics when available (“lead time turun sekitar
  40 %”).  Do not adjust numbers for effect.
* **Anonymity where required** – If a client cannot be named, refer to them
  by industry and context (“klien manufaktur di Jawa Barat”).  Do not invent
  company names or logos.
* **Quotes** – Use direct quotes from clients or team members.  Keep them
  short (one or two sentences) and attribute them (name, role, company if
  permitted).  If no quotes are available, omit the section rather than
  fabricate.

## Phrases & Patterns to Avoid

* “Kami adalah perusahaan logistik terkemuka…” – Overused and unsubstantiated.
* “Solusi holistik terintegrasi end‑to‑end…” – Sounds like jargon.  Replace
  with specific services offered.
* “Memberikan layanan terbaik dengan komitmen tinggi…” – Empty promise.
* “Inovasi tak tertandingi dalam rantai pasok…” – Unless backed by specific
  innovations, avoid this claim.
* “Kepuasan pelanggan adalah prioritas kami…” – Assumes customer viewpoint
  without evidence.  Show proof instead.
* “Kami memahami kebutuhan Anda yang unik…” – Replace with a factual
  statement about evaluating each project individually.

## Anti‑Robot / Anti‑AI Writing Rules

* **No filler paragraphs** – Every sentence must serve a purpose.  Trim
  introductions and conclusions that do not add information.
* **No unnatural connectors** – Avoid phrases like “Moreover,” “Furthermore,”
  “Additionally,” which can reveal AI drafting.  Use natural transitions such
  as “Selain itu,” “Di sisi lain,” or start a new sentence.
* **No synonyms for variety’s sake** – Do not rotate synonyms (e.g. “cargo”,
  “consignment”, “shipment”) unnaturally.  Choose the correct term and stick
  with it in the given context.
* **Avoid hedging** – Phrases like “in conclusion,” “it is important to note”
  signal summary but add no value.  Remove them.

## Anti‑Overclaim Rules

* **Quantify only when verifiable** – If a number cannot be backed by
  operational data, avoid it.  Use qualitative descriptors instead (“banyak
  rute”, “beragam ukuran truk”).
* **No absolutes** – Avoid words like “selalu”, “tidak pernah”, “100 %”, unless
  legally mandated (“kami memastikan 100 % kepatuhan terhadap peraturan
  bea cukai”).
* **Process honesty** – If a process involves risks or delays (e.g. customs
  inspections), mention them and explain how UGC mitigates them rather than
  promising they will not happen.

## Examples of Brochure‑Style Writing to Reject

* “Kami senantiasa berkomitmen untuk memberikan layanan logistik terbaik di
  kelasnya dengan teknologi terkini dan jaringan global terluas.”
* “Sebagai penyedia solusi supply chain end‑to‑end terdepan, kami menawarkan
  paket lengkap dengan harga kompetitif dan keandalan tak tertandingi.”
* “Kami adalah partner strategis yang akan membawa bisnis Anda ke tingkat
  berikutnya.”

Replace statements like these with factual descriptions of what UGC does,
whom it serves and how it adds value.  If a benefit is unique, explain why.
